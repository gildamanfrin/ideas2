Product / Values / People

Framework · Analysing and structuring fashion visual content
Framework

This framework was developed as a grid to analyse and categorise the kinds of visual and content assets fashion brands can use in order to build richer and more articulated forms of storytelling, in an organised and scalable way.

Rather than focusing on isolated outputs or on a generic distinction between campaigns, social media, and editorial content, it proposes a simpler structure based on three recurring dimensions: product, values, and people.

Starting point

A good communication plan — as well as one that is more nuanced and interesting to work on — can balance product valorisation, the expression of brand values, and the presence of the people who shape or surround the brand.

In this sense, the framework works both as an analytical tool and as a design tool: it helps to read existing communication, identify missing areas, and imagine a more layered editorial direction.

Different types of content interact over time to create a broader cultural and visual ecosystem, expanding or contracting according to the brand’s strategic needs and budget constraints.

Use

In teaching, I use this structure to help students move beyond generic content planning and towards more coherent narrative thinking, where each content typology supports a wider strategic direction.

Combinations

The framework is not intended as a rigid taxonomy, but as a modular structure. The different elements it identifies can be combined in multiple ways, allowing brands to construct more articulated forms of storytelling rather than repeating the same logic across all contents.

The way these dimensions interact and shift in balance according to context, medium, and strategic intention provides a way of telling stories rooted in editorial thinking, prioritising what brands have to say, with the goal of directing traffic to proprietary websites and platforms rather than simply responding to the demands of social media algorithms.

In this sense, the framework helps to imagine narrative systems that are recognisable yet not repetitive, where different content types can be organised to support a broader editorial direction without collapsing into uniformity.

Three dimensions

Product

Content centred on the object itself and on its visual or commercial aspects.

This includes still life imagery, detail shots, lookbooks, fashion show content, on-body editorials, and other formats that foreground the commercial and visual aspects of the collection and current season.

Values

Content that expresses what the brand stands for, or the wider world it wants to evoke.

This can include mood-driven visual positioning, cultural references, patronage, or pure editorial and cultural projects such as Prada Frames, Miu Miu Literary Club, or reposts of artworks and design pieces, as seen in strategies such as The Row.

People

Content built around the individuals who shape the brand, like designers, manufacturers or team members, as well as community-based editorial projects.

Designers, team members, artists, collaborators, musicians, muses, and other figures can all become part of the storytelling structure.

Hybrid narrative forms

Product + Values

Content that expresses what the brand stands for while highlighting product characteristics that recur across collections.

This can include craftsmanship stories, special techniques, materials, and ways of making products independent of the current season, as well as branded collaborations with companies from other industries.

Product + People

Products activated through human presence, collaboration, or endorsement.

This includes collaborations with artists or celebrities, whether in the creation of products or as testimonial figures within campaigns and editorials.

Values + People

Content where individuals embody a wider worldview, sensibility, or cultural positioning.

Podcast-like or blog-like formats, interviews, community editorials, and curated series of protagonists can all contribute to building a sense of community around the brand.

Potential outcome

Used in this way, the grid can support the construction of editorial plans that are more varied, more precise, and less dependent on generic content repetition, allowing brands to articulate themselves through richer and more distinct storytelling formats.

Why it matters

Seen this way, storytelling is not a decorative layer added after the product, but a system through which brands organise meaning. The framework helps to understand which content types are already present, which ones are overused, and which ones could expand the brand’s narrative more effectively.
